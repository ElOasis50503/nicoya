Costa Rica Histórica · Transport Series

Crossings

Three fords, three eras — men, machine, and ox-team against moving water

Costa Rica's rivers have always demanded a toll of those who would pass. Before bridges reached the interior, every road met its match at the water's edge — and how a traveler crossed said as much about the age as where they were going.

This small gathering sets three such moments side by side: a barefoot ford on the Atlantic slope, an oxcart team standing fast in a Pacific-side current, and a motorcar nosing through a Guanacaste streambed. Separated by decades and by mode of travel, each is the same old negotiation between traveler and river.

C-01
Magazine Plate · Atlantic Slope

Talamanca Indians Weighted Down With Heavy Stones to Aid in Maintaining Foothold in Crossing a Swiftly Flowing Stream

A page from The National Geographic Magazine, page 214, photograph credited to H. Wimmer. Three men ford a fast mountain stream single file, each shouldering a stone-weighted bundle — ballast carried deliberately to anchor their footing against the current rather than for any cargo purpose.

The accompanying caption observes that the country is threaded with countless rivers draining to both the Atlantic and Pacific, few of them navigable beyond their lower courses — a reminder that in the Talamanca highlands, fording was not a hazard of travel but its everyday condition.

C-02
Postcard · Vista del Campo

Vista del Campo, Costa Rica

Two ox-drawn carretas stand in mid-river, their teams braced against the current while three men ride and walk alongside. The lead cart's wheels are already submerged to the hub; the second team waits its turn on the bank behind. Dense gallery forest closes in on both sides of the watercourse.

The carreta was Costa Rica's working vehicle of the coffee and sugar economy for the better part of a century, and river crossings of exactly this kind — patient, deliberate, the ox-team's weight doing as much as its pull — were a routine feature of getting produce to market before all-weather roads reached the rural interior.

C-03
Photographic Print · Guanacaste

Between Bagares and Liberia

A streamlined sedan — rear-engined, with the swept, beetle-like profile characteristic of mid-century Tatra and Tatraplan touring cars — sits low in a wide, stony streambed somewhere on the road between Bagares and Liberia in Guanacaste. No bridge, no ford markers; the car simply drives the river bottom as the road.

The image carries a pastvu.com archive watermark (item 554569) tagged "TravelerCar," suggesting it circulated as a travel or motoring photograph rather than a studio postcard. The wide cobbled riverbed and dry-season water level point to Guanacaste's seasonal streams, passable by motorcar for a few months and an obstacle for the rest of the year.
