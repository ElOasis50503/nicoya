Máscaras del Diablito

A 500-year-old act of defiance, carved in balsa — and the twenty-five years in which it became Costa Rica's most recognizable souvenir.

Two plates photographed June 2026 — Olympus E-M1X · DxO DeepPRIME XD
One Shop, Then Every Shop

The masks belong to the Boruca (also called Brunca), an Indigenous people of roughly 2,000–2,600 members living in the Reserva Indígena Boruca above the Río Térraba valley in the South Pacific zone — the same broad cultural region, though not the same people, associated with the Diquís stone spheres discussed elsewhere in this archive. Boruca tradition holds that the masks originated during the Spanish Conquest, carved and worn to frighten colonizers into believing the local population was demon-possessed and best left alone. The strategy is remembered as a success: the Boruca count themselves among the few Costa Rican Indigenous groups never fully subjugated by Spanish forces.

"It's not just a business. It's a way to remember who we are." — A Boruca villager, quoted in The Telegraph

That memory is re-enacted every year at the Fiesta de los Diablitos (December 30–January 2), in which masked dancers — the diablitos — engage a man costumed as a bull representing the Spanish, and in a deliberate inversion of the historical record, the devils win. The festival has run, in some form, since the colonial period itself.

A Working Timeline
c. 1490s–1500s

The first masks

Boruca ancestors carve devil-faced masks to convince Spanish forces the population is dangerous and demon-aligned, contributing to the Boruca's status as one of the few groups never fully conquered.

Colonial period – present

The Fiesta de los Diablitos

The masked dance re-enacting resistance to the Spanish is held annually, December 30 – January 2, in Boruca and the neighboring hamlet of Curré.

1980s

From ritual to retail

Don Ismael González begins producing masks specifically for sale, training younger carvers in the technique. The National Museum helps establish a community museum in 1985.

1990s–2000s

Institutional support, brighter palette

INA training introduces vivid acrylic paint technique aimed at tourist appeal, supplementing the older natural-dye tradition. González receives the National Prize for Popular Culture in 2002.

Roughly one generation later

National ubiquity

Masks once sold from a single San José shop now appear in souvenir stalls nationwide — alongside a parallel market in mass-produced, non-Boruca imitations.

Two Diablitos, Compared

Photographed side by side, these two masks show how much room the tradition leaves for individual style — same iconography, two distinct hands.

Plate I

Fire-Horned Diablito

Balsa wood, acrylic · boar-tusk fangs

Red-to-gold gradient horns and a flame motif framing the brow. Turquoise eye surrounds set with painted gold dots echo jaguar rosettes — a common secondary motif layered onto the primary devil form. The forehead ridge is built as five raised, mottled segments, a stylization seen across many contemporary workshops.

Jaguar-Ridge Diablito

Balsa wood, acrylic · boar-tusk fangs

A more rounded, almost cartoon-bright treatment of the eyes sits beneath a continuous jaguar-spotted ridge running down to the snout. The red-banded geometric border under the chin and the contrast against the green ground panel suggest a different workshop hand than Plate I, despite near-identical iconographic vocabulary.

Buying One

If you want the mask to actually mean what it depicts

  • Source matters more than price. The Boruca reserve itself, Boruca-run cooperatives, and galleries that name their artisans (Galería Namú in San José is frequently cited) are the most reliable routes to a mask that actually supports the community of origin.
  • Tourist-hub stalls are a mixed bag. Jacó, Tamarindo, and similar beach towns carry both genuine Boruca work and unrelated mass-produced copies using the same visual vocabulary — ask where a piece was carved if it matters to you.
  • Material is a clue, not a guarantee. Balsa wood (light, easily carved) and cedar are both traditional; either can be genuine or copied.
  • The fangs are often real. Many masks, including both plates above, are fitted with boar tusks rather than carved wooden teeth — a detail worth feeling for.
  • The festival is the source. Visiting Boruca or the neighboring hamlet of Curré during the Fiesta de los Diablitos (Dec 30–Jan 2) puts the object back in its living context — a four-hour drive south from San José, well off the standard tourist circuit.
