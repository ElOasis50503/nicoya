La Negrita

Nuestra Señora de los Ángeles — Santuario y Romería

A small stone figure found on a riverside rock in 1635 became, within two centuries, the patroness of a nation. The Basílica de Nuestra Señora de los Ángeles in Cartago — rebuilt in the years after the 1910 earthquake that levelled its predecessor — remains Costa Rica’s principal site of pilgrimage, drawing millions of romeros each year toward the gilded camarín that houses her image. This catalogue gathers the photographic and postcard record of that shrine and its devotion, independent of any single region, in keeping with the subject’s own stature.

Placa I
Placa I · Plate I

Memorias de Costa Rica — Procesión de la Virgen de los Ángeles

Memories of Costa Rica — Procession of the Virgin of the Angels

Lugar / LocationCosta Rica (calle sin identificar)
Fecha / Date1903
Editor / PublisherJuan Knöhr Hijos, San José

Two images share a single card. In the oval inset, lower left, sits the image itself: La Virgen de los Ángeles, crowned and haloed in a sunburst of gilded rays, raised on a tiered silver pedestal — a studio photograph of the statue alone, isolated against black so that every facet of crown and aureole reads clearly. Beside it, filling two-thirds of the card, a street scene labelled simply Procesión: a crowd packed shoulder to shoulder down a narrow street, umbrellas raised against the sun, balconies on both sides crowded with spectators in white summer dress, a canopy of palm fronds and bunting strung pole to pole above the line of march. Surplices and a portable float bearing the image are visible near the head of the crowd, perhaps a third of the way down the frame.

The card belongs to the “Memorias de Costa Rica” series issued by Juan Knöhr Hijos — a San José trading house better known across the city for its general store than for its sideline in photography. Juan Knöhr was in fact Johann Heinrich Knöhr, a Hamburg-born merchant who had settled in San José by the later nineteenth century and built a retail business, the Almacén Knöhr, that by 1914 occupied its own landmark building at the corner of Avenida Central and Calle Primera. The “Memorias” title runs across dozens of surviving cards — coffee harvests, banana plantations, street and church scenes — issued under one banner as informal advertisements for a country eager to be seen.

Costa Rican postcards before 1907 carried an undivided back, reserved for the recipient’s address alone; anything a sender wanted to add by hand had to be fitted onto the front, in whatever white margin the printer had left unused. That is exactly what happened here: a looping signature — “T. Assmann” — and a second, fainter forwarding note in another hand both crowd the space above the medallion, alongside the printed title. The layout itself argues for an early date in the series’ run, before the postal reform changed how the cards were divided.

One detail is worth a note in passing. The sender’s inscription beside the signature reads, as best it can be made out, a date in 1903 — consistent with a German-surnamed correspondent in a city whose merchant class still included a substantial Hamburg-born contingent. The exact day and month aren’t fully settled, and don’t need to be; 1903 is the figure that matters here. The exact street is equally open — the tiled roofs, wrought-iron balconies, and telegraph lines threading between buildings fit San José’s turn-of-the-century center as readily as Cartago’s own, and nothing in the printed caption settles the question.

Placa II
Placa II · Plate II

El Camarín de Oro — Nave Central de la Basílica

The Golden Camarín — Central Nave of the Basilica

Lugar / LocationCartago, Costa Rica
Fecha / Date21 abril 1956
Formato / FormatColor chrome postcard

The view is taken from the rear pews, looking down the full length of the central nave toward the high altar — the same line of sight a kneeling pilgrim follows on approach. Paired columns wrapped in a cobalt-and-cream diaper pattern alternate with terracotta-red arches, a Moorish-inflected decorative scheme that announces itself immediately as part of the basilica’s post-1910 rebuilding rather than its seventeenth-century origins.

The earthquake of May 4, 1910 destroyed the church that had stood on this site since 1833. The replacement, begun in 1912 and substantially complete by 1930 under architect Luis Llach, fused neo-Byzantine, Gothic, Baroque, and Moorish vocabularies over a reinforced concrete and steel frame — an unusual hybrid that this photograph records in confident, settled form, the decoration evidently long finished rather than newly applied. At the vanishing point sits the gilded camarín: a small domed shrine-chamber raised on its own pedestal, housing the diminutive stone image the basilica was built to protect.

That image is the reason the building exists at all. Tradition holds it was found in 1635 by Juana Pereira, a young woman of La Puebla de los Pardos, on a rock at the edge of this site; when removed, it reappeared there, twice, and a shrine followed. In 1824 the figure — popularly La Negrita, for the stone’s dark hue — was declared patroness of Costa Rica. Every August since, the romería has brought an estimated 1.5 to 2 million pilgrims toward this altar — making it, by some counts, the fourth-largest Christian pilgrimage in the world — many covering the final stretch of the aisle on their knees.

The card’s reverse documents its own production in full. It is a “Natural Color Card from Kodachrome,” number C4851, A Mike Roberts Color Production of Berkeley, California — the operation founded in 1949 by photographer Mike Roberts, later known as “America’s Postcard King,” who supplied Kodachrome-based postcards to distributors across the United States and abroad. The Costa Rican distributor was Carlos Federspiel & Co. S.A., trading as the Librería Universal — a San José stationery and book shop founded in 1926 by the German-Costa Rican merchant Carlos Federspiel Ullman and his wife, María Kreutzwald, and still operating today as the department-store chain Universal. The photograph is credited to Herbert Lanks, an American travel photographer who had documented the Pan-American Highway through Central America for Harry Franck’s books in the late 1930s and 1940s and went on to supply images to several Mike Roberts postcard lines across the Americas.

The figure in the foreground pew, hooded in blue-green and turned fully away from the camera, is not posed for it. She anchors a tourist-trade postcard — manufactured for circulation, captionless on its face — to an unbroken, ordinary present-tense act of devotion. A handwritten note dates and addresses the card precisely: sent 21 de abril de 1956 via Correo Aéreo, under a 35-céntimos Costa Rican airmail stamp, to Mrs. Earl Brown at 1870 Harding Street, Clearwater, Florida. The sender, signing only “Dan,” reports that Costa Rica’s climate reminded him of springtime back home and that the coffee trees were everywhere in bloom — ordinary mid-century tourist correspondence, which is exactly what gives its date such authority.

Placa III · Plate III

La Fachada Principal — Vista Exterior de la Basílica

The Main Façade — Exterior View of the Basilica

Lugar / LocationCartago, Costa Rica
Fecha / Date1956
Formato / FormatColor chrome postcard

The view that the pilgrim sees on approach, before climbing the steps into the nave recorded in Plate II: the full façade from the garden walkway, twin red domes flanking a taller central one, the whole composition crowned by a cross-bearing lantern. Beneath the great rose window of the gable, a bank of narrow Gothic lancets admits light to the choir loft; white sculpted figures — angels, to judge by their wings — stand in niches along the roofline, the same ornamental vocabulary seen from inside in the gilded camarín. A formal rose garden and a paved promenade lead the eye straight to the central portal, with a low perimeter balustrade separating the grounds from the street.

Paired with Plate II, this card supplies the building’s exterior to that photograph’s interior — the same post-1910 reconstruction, the same Moorish-Byzantine vocabulary, seen this time from the plaza rather than the rear pews. Visitors are scattered across the foreground: a couple walking the central path, a figure seated on a bench, a few more gathered near the entrance steps. Along the side street, several rounded, chrome-trimmed cars — a panel wagon and a sedan among them — sit parked at the curb.

The card’s reverse, since examined, settles the publisher question outright: the same “Natural Color Card from Kodachrome” mark, A Mike Roberts Color Production of Berkeley, California, and the same “Publicado por Carlos Federspiel & Co. S.A., Librería Universal, San José Costa Rica” imprint as Plate II, this time under the catalogue number C4612 and a bilingual caption — Basilica of our Lady of the Angels, Protector of Costa Rica — in the same typeface and layout. The two views are unquestionably siblings from the same publishing program.

This exemplar was never mailed — no message, no stamp, no postmark — so it carries no date of its own the way Plate II’s airmail correspondence does. What it shares with Plate II instead is the publisher and the production run, which is enough to place both views in 1956 without needing to fix a day. The catalogue numbers — C4612 here, C4851 for Plate II — confirm they belong to the same series; which of the two was printed first isn’t a question worth chasing further here.
